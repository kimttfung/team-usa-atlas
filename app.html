What Atlas Analyzes

Team USA Atlas is a fan-centric analyst dashboard for the hometown geography of Team USA's roster. Around 5,000 athletes, all 50 states plus DC and the Virgin Islands, summer and winter sports, Olympic and Paralympic — surfaced through one consistent equal-frame view.

The unit of analysis is state (and within state, city hub). Counts are aggregate hometown participation totals, deduplicated per athlete so multi-discipline competitors aren't double-counted. Atlas does not load year, cycle, or per-Games timelines — it surfaces a current-roster footprint, not a historical archive. Every model-driven insight is paired with an Evidence Used block listing the data behind the prose.

Why This Matters

Geography tells a story raw counts and medal tables don't. Where athletes are from is a window into the communities, regions, and climates that may be associated with Team USA representation today — and the patterns are often very different across summer and winter, and across Olympic and Paralympic, programs.

Atlas is built around two beliefs: that Paralympic representation deserves the same surface as Olympic (Parity Lens is a top-level page, not a sub-tab), and that aggregate hometown patterns can be explored honestly without ever trading on individual athlete identity. The product never surfaces names, photos, ages, gender, medals, finish times, or per-event scores — only the geography.

How Gemini Is Used

Gemini is a writing layer, not a reasoning layer. The app computes every count, ranking, and roster filter first; only after that does gemini-3.1-flash-lite turn those compact, app-authored facts into a short, structured insight.

Where Gemini Runs

  • Atlas Regional Insight — a six-bullet readout of the current map view (filters + selected state) that names the leading state, top hometown hubs, season profile, and a few geography-aware observations in conditional language.
  • Sport Insight — snapshot, concentration, hometown hubs, and program mix for the selected sport.
  • Parity Insight — Paralympic representation, share floor, and dual-program presence under the active view.
  • Comparison Insight — multi-section narrative for two states (at a glance, where they're alike, where they differ, most distinct contrast).
  • Ask the Analyst — free-text questions that aren't a direct chip match are sent to Gemini with a small payload of pre-computed aggregate facts.

How a Gemini Call Flows

  1. Compute first. The app derives the numbers — totals, share percentages, top-N lists, season splits — directly from the loaded roster.
  2. Build a compact context. A small JSON payload (typically a few KB) is assembled with just the facts the model needs to write a useful sentence. No raw data, no athlete-level rows, no names.
  3. Call gemini-3.1-flash-lite. Via Google's @google/genai SDK with responseMimeType: application/json, an explicit responseSchema per task, temperature: 0.2, topP: 0.8, and thinkingLevel: MINIMAL for low-latency responses.
  4. Validate the response. A server-side validator checks the shape, array lengths, per-field word caps, and scans for banned phrasing. Anything that fails any check triggers the deterministic local insight instead.
  5. Render with a typewriter reveal. The card paints word-by-word so the experience feels alive. The same animation plays whether the prose came from Gemini or from the local fallback — the surface is intentionally identical so the experience never breaks.

Why a Local Fallback Exists

The Gemini API is the writing layer; the deterministic templates are the safety net. If the API key is missing, the network is offline, the schema check fails, or a banned phrase is detected, the local insight renders without interruption — same typography, same animation, same evidence panel — so the dashboard is fully functional with or without a live Gemini connection.

Where It Runs

The production deployment lives on Google Cloud Run (us-central1) as a managed, scale-to-zero serverless service: Node 20 + Express containerised by Google Cloud Build directly from source, served over Google-managed HTTPS. The Gemini API key is injected as a Cloud Run service-config environment variable — encrypted at rest by Google, never bundled into the image and never sent to the browser. Local development boots the same Express runtime so behaviour is identical end-to-end.

Responsible AI Guardrails

Atlas is shaped end-to-end by the responsible-AI requirements of the challenge: conditional phrasing only, equal-frame Olympic and Paralympic, no individual outputs, and structured-output validation on every single Gemini call.

  • Conditional language only. Insights use "could suggest," "may reflect," "is associated with," "historically aligns with" — never "causes," "produces," "guarantees," or "because of."
  • Banned-phrase scan. A server-side regex checks every Gemini response for ranking, prediction, and causation language — best, worst, champions, winners, medals, medalists, podium, finish time, score, causes, leads to, results in, because of climate, pipeline, talent factory. Any match triggers the deterministic fallback.
  • No NIL, no individual outputs. Athlete names, photos, ages, gender, medals, finish times, and per-event scores are dropped during data cleaning. Gemini never sees them and never writes about them.
  • Compact aggregate facts only. Each Gemini request carries a small JSON payload of pre-computed totals — never raw participation rows, never the full data set.
  • Equal-frame Olympic + Paralympic. Both programs share every surface; Parity Lens is a top-level page, not a sub-tab.
  • Server-side key. The Gemini API key lives in the local server's environment (.env) and is never sent to the browser.
  • Schema-enforced output. Every Gemini call uses an explicit responseSchema; the local validator re-checks shape and per-field word caps before render.
  • Out-of-scope questions decline politely. Ask the Analyst rephrases identity-seeking, performance-seeking, or causal-claim prompts toward what the roster geography can actually answer — and declines outright if it can't.

Allowed vs. Disallowed Language

Use These

  • "Could suggest"
  • "May reflect"
  • "Is associated with"
  • "Historically aligns with"
  • "Represented" / "appears" / "concentrates"
  • "Footprint" / "composition"
  • "Pattern, not prediction"
  • "Aggregate hometown participation"
  • "Equal-frame view"
  • "Share of national"

Avoid These

  • "Best" / "produces" / "owns"
  • "Winner" / "successful"
  • "Improving" / "underperforming"
  • "Predicts" / "guarantees performance"
  • "Because of climate" / "talent factory"
  • Athlete names, photographs, biographies
  • Athlete ages, heights, gender, demographics
  • Medals, finish times, scores, predictions
  • Per-capita / per-1M / population-normalised metrics
  • Year / cycle growth or trend claims

How Counts Work

Two Counting Modes

Unique athlete count: one athlete counted once, regardless of how many sports they appear in. Used by the Atlas choropleth, KPI tiles, and most state-level surfaces.

Athlete-sport participation count: one row per athlete-sport relationship. Multi-discipline athletes contribute multiple rows. Used by Sport Explorer breakdowns so cross-discipline athletes show up under each sport they compete in.

Dual-classified athletes (Olympic and Paralympic) appear in both program splits, but they still count once in unique-athlete totals — which is why Olympic + Paralympic split totals can sum higher than the national unique total.

State total
Unique athletes whose hometown resolves to a given state.
Hometown total
Unique athletes whose hometown resolves to a given city hub.
Olympic / Paralympic split
Unique athletes per program. Dual-program athletes appear in both splits but count once in the unique total.
Summer / Winter split
Unique athletes per season. Cross-season athletes appear in both splits but count once in the unique total.
Paralympic share
Paralympic athletes ÷ total athletes for the same scope (state or hometown). Reported as a percentage. Describes composition: what fraction of the visible athletes are Paralympic.
Balance index
min(Olympic, Paralympic) ÷ max(Olympic, Paralympic) for the same scope. A 0–1 ratio where 1.00 = perfectly balanced and 0.00 = totally lopsided.
Share of national
Scope total ÷ national unique-athlete total. Reported as a percentage; no population denominator is joined.

Known Limitations

  • Hometown ≠ training location. Many athletes are listed as being from somewhere different from where they trained or currently live; the "hub" view is biographical, not facility-driven.
  • Demographic signals (age, height, gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic background) are not joined. Patterns therefore over-represent factors that correlate with public hometown reporting.
  • Climate context is descriptive only. Atlas does not test, model, or imply a relationship between weather and performance.
  • Year and cycle trend analysis are out of scope — the cleaned roster is an aggregate snapshot, not a time series. Growth or decline statements should not be drawn from it.
  • Gemini writes from the compact context the app passes it. It does not retrieve from the open web at answer time.

Evidence Used

Every chip, KPI, and analyst answer surfaces an Evidence Used block listing the underlying data the answer was composed from. The notation file · 1,234 rows means the answer was composed from 1,234 rows of that source as currently loaded — no other data is consulted.
